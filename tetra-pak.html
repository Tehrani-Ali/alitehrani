Field Service & Technical Sales Engineer

Job History & Context

My career began at Tetra Pak — one of the world's largest food processing and packaging companies — where I started as a junior service engineer and grew into a Global Service Engineer over seven years. This was a formative period that shaped every engineering skill I have today.

I was deployed to remote international sites across the Middle East, often alone, in countries with different languages and cultures. This was a time before reliable internet — communication with the technical hub in Sweden was limited, and I had to be completely self-sufficient in the field. I was responsible for the full production line: pasteurizers, valve clusters, sterilizers, aseptic tanks, CIP systems, and packaging machines.

The stakes were high. Dairy products cannot be held — contaminated milk cannot be drained into the environment and cannot be stored indefinitely. Every hour of downtime had direct consequences for the customer's business and for public safety. This environment taught me to think fast, solve problems independently, and always put the customer's production first.

Over time, I went deep into every system — pumps, valves, piping, pressure, temperature, PLC/SCADA — until I could diagnose and resolve any issue independently. This technical depth earned the trust of both the company and our customers, and naturally expanded my role into consulting, spare parts planning, preventive maintenance design, operator training, and technical documentation.

I was promoted from Junior Service Engineer to Global Service Engineer — the highest field recognition at Tetra Pak — and delivered projects for some of the world's most demanding manufacturers.

Story 01
Flexible Commissioning Under Pressure
Situation
Arrived at a remote customer site to commission a full production line — pasteurizer, valve cluster, sterilizer, aseptic tank, CIP, and packaging machines. Upon arrival, the customer was not ready: steam supply had issues, electrical infrastructure was incomplete, and utilities were not in place. Dairy product could not be held or wasted. The standard commissioning sequence (box opening → positioning → installation → hardware/I-O check → utility verification → commissioning → water test → test production → QC → commercial production) could not proceed as planned.
Action
Rather than waiting or escalating, I restructured the entire project plan on the spot based on what the customer could realistically deliver. I bypassed the steps that depended on unavailable utilities and instead floated water through the full line — from reception all the way through to packaging — to test system functions, flow paths, valve logic, and mechanical integrity. This kept the project moving, identified issues early, and gave the customer time to resolve their utility problems in parallel.
Result
The project stayed on track. When the customer resolved their utility issues, the remaining commissioning steps were completed efficiently because the system had already been verified with water. No production time was lost unnecessarily and the customer's confidence in the process — and in me — was significantly strengthened.

Story 02
Cross-Supplier Integration with No Internet
Situation
On an international site, an aseptic tank from a third-party supplier could not communicate with the Tetra Pak packaging machine. There was no internet. Communication with the technical hub in Sweden was unreliable and time-delayed. Tetra Pak's support team only covered Tetra Pak equipment — the other supplier's system was outside their scope. The customer had no technical expertise to address the issue. Physically touching the other supplier's machine carried significant risk — both technically and contractually.
Action
I tracked down a local representative or field engineer from the other supplier and built enough trust and rapport to get their technical cooperation — despite having no formal authority over them. I gathered the integration requirements step by step from that engineer. Rather than attempting the integration myself and risking damage to their equipment, I trained the customer's own operators to follow the precise sequence I had obtained. I coordinated the process remotely, step by step, acting as the bridge between the two suppliers and the customer.
Result
The aseptic tank and packaging machine were successfully integrated without any direct physical intervention on the third-party system. The production line became operational. The customer was empowered with the knowledge to handle similar issues in the future, and the relationship between all parties was strengthened.

Story 03
Localized Training & Simplified Documentation
Situation
Many customers had frontline staff — operators, technicians, and engineers — who could not operate the machines confidently and could not read English. Standard Tetra Pak technical documentation was complex, text-heavy, and contained no practical examples. This created a dependency on Tetra Pak field support for routine tasks and reduced the customer's ability to operate independently.
Action
I translated key documentation into the local language to make it accessible. I then redesigned the training program for three distinct audiences — operators, technicians, and engineers — each receiving the right depth of knowledge for their role. Most importantly, I enriched the documents with real-world examples and photographs attached directly to the relevant procedural steps, turning abstract technical instructions into something anyone could follow. This was something standard Tetra Pak documentation never provided.
Result
Customers' staff became confident and self-sufficient in operating and maintaining the machines. Dependency on Tetra Pak field support for routine issues dropped significantly. Production efficiency and uptime improved. The customer relationship deepened — they saw me not just as a service engineer, but as a trusted partner invested in their success.
